* Law of Total Variance
#+PROPERTY: header-args:R :prologue "source(\"prelude/test_df.R\");" :colnames yes
#+begin_quote
[[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_total_variance][Wikipedia: Law of total variance]]

'''The law of total variance''' is a fundamental result in _probability theory_ that expresses the variance of a random variable {{mvar|Y}} in terms of its conditional variances and conditional means given another random variable {{mvar|X}}. Informally, it states that the overall variability of {{mvar|Y}} can be split into an “unexplained” component (the average of within-group variances) and an “explained” component (the variance of group means).

Formally, if {{mvar|X}} and {{mvar|Y}} are _random variables_ on the same _probability space_, and {{mvar|Y}} has finite _variance_, then:

\[
\operatorname{Var}(Y) = \operatorname{E}[\operatorname{Var}(Y \mid X)] + \operatorname{Var}(\operatorname{E}[Y \mid X])
\]

This identity is also known as the '''variance decomposition formula''', the '''conditional variance formula''', the '''law of iterated variances''', or colloquially as '''Eve’s law''',<ref/> in parallel to the “Adam’s law” naming for the _law of total expectation_.
#+end_quote

** Base Conditional Variance
In our simulator measurement representation:
- Variable \(X\) is the control parameter (e.g., baseParameter)
- Variable \(Y\) represents observations after the simulation and measurement process
- In the most basic case, \(Y\) can be the measurement result itself (denoted as \(H\), e.g., baseLog.agent_time_enableds)

To simplify the computation, we assume \(H\) has:
- Mean: \(\mu_x = E[H | X=x]\)
- Universal variance: \(\sigma^2 = \sigma_x^2 = \text{Var}(H | X=x)\)

** Same Control Parameter Difference Variance
We define our second variance for \( Y \leftarrow (H - H') | X = x \), where:
- \(H\) and \(H'\) are two replica observations
- Both are conditioned by the same \(X=x\)

In this case:
- \( E[Y | X=x] = 0 \) (expected difference is zero)
- \( \text{Var}(Y | X=x) = 2 \text{Var}(H | X=x) = 2\sigma_x^2 \)
- \( \text{Var}(Y) = E[\text{Var}(Y|X=x)] = 2\sigma^2 \)

** Random Control Parameter Difference Variance
Our third form of variance is \( Y \leftarrow H(X) - H'(X') \), where \(X\) and \(X'\) are independent parameters.

Using the law of total variance:
\[ \text{Var}(Y) = E[\text{Var}(Y | X, X')] + \text{Var}(E[ Y | X, X']) \]

Since \(X\) and \(X'\) follow the same distribution and are i.i.d.:

For the first part of \(\text{Var}(Y)\):

Given conditional variance \( \text{Var}(Y | X, X') = \sigma_X^2 + \sigma_{X'}^2 \)

Then \( E[\text{Var}(Y | X, X')] \leftarrow E[\sigma_X^2] + E[\sigma_{X'}^2] = 2 E[\sigma_X^2] = 2\sigma^2 \)

For the second part of \(\text{Var}(Y)\):

Given conditional mean \( E[ Y | X, X' ] \leftarrow \mu_X - \mu_{X'} \)

Then \( \text{Var}(E[Y | X, X']) \leftarrow \text{Var}(\mu_X - \mu_{X'}) = 2 \text{Var}(\mu_X) \)

Thus \( \text{Var}(Y) = 2\sigma^2 + 2\text{Var}(\mu_X)\)

**Key Result:** The variance difference between random control parameter and same control parameter is exactly the **impact variance**.

I define **impact variance** as the component of \(Y\) measurements that depends on the conditioning variable \(X\). This represents how much the control parameter \(X\) actually influences the output.

*Note:* The theory should still hold if we relax the universal variance assumption by replacing \(\sigma^2\) with \( E[\sigma_X^2]\) (requires formal proof).

** Comparison Summary

The three datasets demonstrate different scenarios:

1. **Original dataset**: Real simulation data with moderate parameter influence
2. **Sinusoidal function**: Strong non-linear parameter influence (\\(h(x) = \sin(x) + \text{noise}\\))
3. **Constant function**: No parameter influence (\\(h(x) = C + \text{noise}\\))

The impact variance should be:
- **Highest** for the sinusoidal function (strong parameter dependency)
- **Near zero** for the constant function (no parameter dependency) 
- **Moderate** for the original dataset (real-world parameter influence)

This demonstrates how the variance decomposition method can quantify the degree to which control parameters actually influence experimental outcomes.
